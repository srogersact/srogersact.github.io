Introduction to Compound Sentences

In Grammar Effects you can learn how to analyze sentences.

In Grammar Effects...
  • When you analyze a sentence, you will break it into simple sentences.
Analyzing a compound sentence is easy!

 A-1   Analyzing a Compound Sentence

Here is a compound sentence:

A robin is a bird, and a cat is a mammal.

This sentence can be analyzed as two sentences.
You simply...

  • Change the comma into a period.
  • Take out the conjunction"And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or.".
  • Start your sentences with capitals.

A robin is a bird. A cat is a mammal.

That is super easy, don't you think?

Here is another simple example:

A horse is a kind of mammal, and"And" is a conjunction. a crocodile is a kind of reptile.

Again, you just...

  • Change the comma into a period.
  • Take out the conjunction"And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or.".
  • Start your sentences with capitals.

A horse is a kind of mammal. A crocodile is a kind of reptile.

 C-1   Composing a Compound Sentence

In Grammar Effects you can learn how to compose sentences. It's just the opposite of analyzing.

In Grammar Effects...
  • To compose a sentence, you join simple sentences together. This will make a more complicated sentence.

Here are two sentences:

Mammals breathe with lungs. Fish breathe with gills.

These sentences can be composed as one compound sentence.

Here is what you do:

  • Change the period at the end of the first sentence into a comma.
  • Add a conjunction."And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or."
  • Make sure you don't have extra capitals in your sentence.

Mammals breathe with lungs, and"And" is a conjunction. fish breathe with gills.

That's super easy, too, don't you think?

Here is another example:

Frogs often have color patterns that make them hard to see. Some insects have shapes that make them look like sticks or leaves.

These are longer sentences. Do they make you nervous? Just remember these simple directions:

  • Change the period at the end of the first sentence into a comma.
  • Add a conjunction"And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or." after the comma.
  • Make sure you don't have unnecessary capitals in your sentence.

Frogs often have color patterns that make them hard to see, and some insects have shapes that make them look like sticks or leaves.

We changed the period to a comma.

We added "and."

 A-2   Analyzing Compound Sentences with Three or More Clauses

Sometimes a sentence has three or more clausesA clause is a part of a sentence that can stand by itself as a complete sentence.:

A monkey is a mammal that climbs in trees, a dolphin is a mammal that swims in the ocean, and a bat is a mammal that flies through the air.

  • The first clause is A monkey is a mammal that climbs in trees.
  • The second clause is A dolphin is a mammal that swims in the ocean.
  • The third clause is A bat is a mammal that flies through the air.

Now it is easy to see that the compound sentence was really just a list of sentences.

A compound sentence with three or more clauses is easy to analyze:

  • Change the commas into periods.
  • Remove the conjunction.
  • Make sure your sentences start with capitals.

A monkey is a mammal that climbs in trees. A dolphin is a mammal that swims in the ocean. A bat is a mammal that flies in the air.

A sentence can also have four, five, or more sentences.
If you focus on just one of those sentences at a time, it will be easier to understand.

Now let's think about why writers use compound sentences. Did you notice anything about the three sentences that were joined together to make one sentence?

  • The first clause is about a monkey. This clause tells us what a monkey is (a mammal) what it does (climbs) and where it is found (in trees).
  • The second clause is about a dolphin. This clause tells us what a dolphin is (also a mammal) what it does (swims) and where it is found (in the ocean).
  • The third clause is about a bat. This clause tells us what a bat is (this is a mammal, too!) and where it is found (in the air).
Each of the sentences is about a mammal. Each of the sentences tells us the same three things about a different mammal:
  1. the name of the mammal,
  2. what the mammal does,
  3. where the mammal is found.
The sentences compare the three mammals. Compound sentences are often used to to show that we are comparing things.

 C-2   Composing Compound Sentences with Three or More Clauses

Sometimes you have three or more sentences that go together:

Whales migrate between the tropics and the arctic. Salmon migrate between streams and oceans. Elk migrate between mountains and valleys.

Do you see why these sentences go together?

  • The sentences compare three kinds of animalWhales, salmon, and elk. that migrate To migrate means to move from one place to another when the season changes..
  • The sentences also compare three kinds of migration.Between the tropics and the arctic, between streams and oceans, and between mountains and valleys.

It depends on your point of view.

It is easy to compose a compound sentence using these three sentences. Here is what you do:

  • Change the periods at the ends of the sentences into commas—except for the period at the end of the very last sentence.
  • Add a conjunction"And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or." before the very last sentence.
  • Make sure you don't have capitals in the middle of your sentence if they don't need to be there.

Whales migrate between the tropics and the arctic, salmon migrate between streams and oceans, and elk migrate between mountains and valleys.

Notice that we use commas where the first two periods went.

Use the Oxford Comma:

  • In this work you will always put a comma before the conjunction"And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or." at the end of a list.
  • This is what is called the "Oxford comma."
  • Some people prefer to leave out the Oxford comma. That's perfectly okay...
    • ❗ ...BUT you will have to use the Oxford comma in the "Try It Yourself" exercises. Otherwise, they won't work.

Using this grammar effect, you can break some pretty large sentences into smaller sentences that are easier to understand.

 A-4   Marking a Contrast

So far we have been writing compound sentences that use "and" as the conjunction. These sentences have been showing how things are similar.

Now we will think about compound sentences that emphasize how things are different.

Frogs have soft, moist skin, but lizards have dry, scaly skin.

This compound sentence is showing how frogs are different from lizards.

It may be easier to notice the contrast if we analyze the sentence into its clauses:

Frogs have soft, moist skin. However, lizards have dry, scaly skin.

Did you notice that word, "However"? We can use it to replace the word, "but."

Use "However" to replace "but."

  • In this work, you will not start a sentence with a conjunction"And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or.".
  • The words "but" and "However" are used to show contrast.
  • Replace the word "but" with "However" at the beginning of a sentence.
  • "However" always has to have a comma right after it.
❗ You will have to replace "but" with "However,..." in the "Try It Yourself" exercises. Otherwise, the exercises won't work.

 C-4   Making a Contrast

The two sentences below make a contrast:

Most reptiles have four legs. However, snakes do not have legs.

The contrast is between most reptiles and snakes (which are a type of reptile).

The word "However" is used to show the contrast. If we join the two sentences, we will use "but" as the conjunction. The word "but" shows contrast.

Most reptiles have four legs, but snakes do not have legs.

"However" and "but" have the same grammar effect of showing contrast.

  • "However" is used at the beginning of a sentence to show contrast.
  • The word "but" is used as a conjunction in the middle of a sentence to show contrast.

Don't use a comma after "but."

  • "However" at the beginning of a sentence has a comma after it.
  • The word "but" does not have a comma after it.
  • The comma comes before the word "but."
❗ You will have to put a comma before "but" in the "Try It Yourself" exercises—not after. Otherwise, they won't work.

 C-5   Composing a Longer Contrast Sentence

The second of these two sentences is pretty long, isn't it?

A boy or a girl can climb up a birch tree and make it bend to the ground until they jump off. However, ice storms can load birch trees with so much ice that they bend to the ground for good.

We will do the same things as usual:

  • Change the period at the end of the first sentence into a comma.
  • Add a conjunction"And" is a conjunction--and so are "but" and "or." between the sentences. In this case we will use "but."
  • Make sure the comma is before the conjunction.
  • Make sure there is not an unnecessary capital in the middle of the sentence.

A boy or a girl can climb up a birch tree and make it bend to the ground until they jump off, but ice storms can load birch trees with so much ice that they bend to the ground for good.
