Parts of Sentences:
A Trip Across Washington

This sentence demonstrates how conjunctions such as and, but, and or are marked.

We can take an imaginary trip across our state. The journey starts in the morning. Near the town of Aberdeen we board a plane. As the plane ascends, you see the coastline. In the north the coast is rugged and rocky. There are thick evergreen forests right next to the shore. In the south there are sandy beaches.

The plane flies toward the Olympic Mountains. The tallest peaks are covered with snow. You see gently rolling hills south of the mountains.

Soon you fly over a long, narrow body of water. George Vancouver explored this area by ship in the late 1700’s. He generously named it Puget Sound to honor an aide with him named Peter Puget. In the late 1700’s many Native Americans lived in the Puget Sound area. Now there are big cities along the shores of Puget Sound, and there are many cars and trucks on the many highways.

Beyond the cities you can see farms among the forests. This part of the state is called the “Western Lowlands” because it is in the western part of the state and because it is near sea level. Several rivers flow through the Western Lowlands and into the Pacific Ocean.

Farther east, where the cities disappear, you reach the Cascade Mountains. In the middle of the state, the Cascade Mountains rise steeply like a tall wall. In the Cascade mountains, snow covers many of the peaks all year long because they are so high.

The land changes suddenly on the east side of the Cascades because it is so much drier. On the east side of the Cascades you can sometimes see green farmland among the dry grasslands. In this dry part of the state, farmers usually have to irrigate their land so their crops will grow.
